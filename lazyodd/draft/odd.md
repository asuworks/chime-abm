## 1. Purpose and Patterns

### 1.1 Purpose

The CHIME ABM was designed as a **virtual laboratory** to investigate the dynamics of hazardous weather communication and decision making during hurricane threats. It examines how information flow through the modern information environment affects household evacuation decisions. [source: README.md:3; How To Use CHIME.md:1-3] {DOC_STATED}

The higher-level purpose is **explanation and theoretical exploration**: understanding how the modern information environment -- with multiple, sometimes conflicting information sources -- shapes household-level hurricane evacuation decisions. [source: README.md:3] {DOC_STATED}

The model is grounded in the **Protective Action Decision Model (PADM)** framework (Lindell & Perry, 2012), which posits that protective action decisions arise from a sequential process of information reception, threat perception, protective action search, and protective action decision-making. The CHIME ABM operationalizes this framework by creating heterogeneous citizen-agents who receive information from multiple channels, form interpreted forecasts, assess risk, and ultimately decide whether to evacuate. [source: README.md:3] {DOC_STATED}

The model was developed to address the research gap identified by Morss et al. (2017): the modern information environment has fundamentally changed how people receive, process, and act on hazardous weather information. Traditional communication models assumed a single authoritative information channel; today, citizens receive weather information from official forecasters, traditional broadcast media, social media aggregators, and their personal social networks, each with varying levels of accuracy, timeliness, and trust. The CHIME ABM captures this complexity by representing each of these information channels as distinct agent types with their own behaviors.

Users can modify parameters to explore the relative effects of:

- Weighting of different information types (forecast, evacuation orders, environmental cues) via `forc-weight`, `evac-weight`, `envc-weight`
- Inclusion of census demographic information via toggle switches
- Different hurricanes affecting different regions (9 storm scenarios across 3 regions)
- Perfect versus imperfect forecasts (IDEAL vs. REAL vs. BAD forecast files)

[source: How To Use CHIME.md:1-3; CHIME.nlogo BehaviorSpace experiments] {DOC_STATED}

### 1.2 Patterns

The model does not produce a single verifiable outcome. Instead, output consists of the timing and location of household evacuations, evaluated against multiple dimensions of evacuation behavior. [source: CHIME.nlogo:2373-2516, Save-Global-Evac-Statistics] {CODE_VERIFIED}

The primary output patterns are:

1. **Evacuation rates by exposure zone**: Percentage of agents evacuated among coastal residents within the 64-kt wind radius, inland residents within the 64-kt wind radius, coastal residents within the 34-kt wind radius (but outside 64-kt), inland residents within the 34-kt wind radius (but outside 64-kt), coastal residents outside all wind radii, and inland residents outside all wind radii -- yielding six distinct categories of spatial exposure.

2. **Evacuation timing relative to landfall**: A histogram of evacuation decisions binned in 6-hour intervals, spanning from 90 hours before landfall to 18 hours after landfall. This temporal distribution captures the dynamics of when households decide to evacuate as the storm approaches.

3. **Official order timing**: When officials in each county issued evacuation orders relative to storm landfall, providing context for the institutional response timeline.

4. **Sensitivity to information weights**: BehaviorSpace experiments systematically sweep `forc-weight`, `evac-weight`, and `envc-weight` across their full ranges, enabling analysis of how the relative importance of different information channels affects aggregate evacuation outcomes. These experiments use 100 repetitions per parameter combination to capture stochastic variability.

### 2.9 Spatial Scale

The model operates on a GIS-derived raster grid with world extent from -100 to 100 on the x-axis and -75 to 75 on the y-axis, yielding a 201 x 151 patch grid. [source: CHIME.nlogo:3005-3030] {CODE_VERIFIED}

Grid cell size is variable, derived from the GIS data envelope for the selected region. The approximate resolution is 0.1 degrees per grid cell, corresponding to roughly 6 nautical miles. {INFERRED from GIS envelope calculations}

Three geographic regions are available for simulation:

- **Florida**: Covers the Florida peninsula and surrounding waters
- **Gulf**: Covers Gulf Coast states from Texas to Florida
- **Gulf+Southeast**: Extends coverage to include Southeast Atlantic states

The coordinate system is projected from geographic lat/lon coordinates to NetLogo grid coordinates using the GIS extension. All agent positions, distances, and spatial calculations operate in the projected grid coordinate space. The `scale` global variable stores the conversion factor between grid cells and nautical miles, and `grid-cell-size` stores the conversion factor between grid cells and degrees.

### 2.10 Temporal Scale

The fundamental time step is **1 tick = 1 hour**. [source: CHIME.nlogo:231-309] {CODE_VERIFIED}

A typical simulation run spans 100 to 140 ticks (hours), depending on the selected storm scenario. The simulation begins when the hurricane first enters the model domain and terminates when the hurricane exits or has passed through the domain.

The citizen-agent decision module does not execute every tick for every agent. Each citizen-agent runs its Decision-Module every `decision-module-interval` ticks (default approximately 12 hours), creating a staggered and heterogeneous decision-making schedule. This interval is adaptive and changes during the simulation based on the agent's current risk assessment (see Section 7.7).

Forecast advisories from the forecaster are published at intervals matching historical NHC advisory timing, typically every 6 hours, and are interpolated to hourly resolution for use by other agents.

## 3. Process Overview and Scheduling

Each time step (1 hour), the following processes execute in this exact order:

1. **Move-Hurricane** -- The hurricane visualization agent advances to the next position from the interpolated best-track data. The hurricane's current intensity, wind radii (34-kt and 64-kt in four quadrants), and geographic position are updated to the values corresponding to the current tick.

2. **Publish-Forecasts** (asked of forecasters) -- The forecaster agent publishes the current NHC-style advisory matched to the simulation time. If the current tick corresponds to a new advisory release time, the forecast is updated; otherwise, the previous advisory remains current.

3. **Publish-New-Mental-Model** (reporter) -- The current discrete forecast advisory is interpolated to hourly resolution, creating an hourly forecast track with intensity, position (x, y), cone of uncertainty, and wind radii for each future hour. This interpolated product is what broadcasters and aggregators receive.

4. **Coastal-Patches-Alerts** -- Each coastal patch evaluates whether the forecasted storm will arrive within `earliest` hours, whether the patch falls within the forecast cone of uncertainty, and whether the forecast intensity exceeds `wind-threshold`. If all conditions are met, the patch sets `alerts = 1`.

5. **Issue-Alerts** (asked of officials whose county has alerted patches) -- Officials in counties containing patches with `alerts = 1` issue evacuation orders by setting `orders = 1` and recording `when-issued`.

6. **Broadcasters update** -- All broadcaster agents receive the interpolated forecast from the Publish-New-Mental-Model reporter and update their `broadcast` variable.

7. **Aggregators update** -- Each aggregator agent has a 1/3 probability of updating its `info` variable with the current interpolated forecast. This probabilistic updating represents the uneven and sometimes delayed nature of social media information propagation.

8. **Citizen-agent decision cycle** (asked of all citizen-agents):
   - If the citizen-agent has **NOT** evacuated: check if it is the agent's turn to run the decision module (`decision-module-turn` reaches `decision-module-interval`). If yes, execute the **Decision-Module** (see Section 7.7). If not the agent's turn, increment `decision-module-turn` and skip.
   - If the citizen-agent has **ALREADY** evacuated: the agent still collects and processes information via **Process-Forecasts** so that its `interpreted-forecast` is available to social network connections who query it.

9. **Color updates** -- Visual updates for agent display on the NetLogo interface. Evacuated agents are colored orange; other agents are colored based on their current risk level.

10. **Clock update** -- The simulation clock `[day, hour]` is updated from the best-track data to reflect the current calendar time.

11. **Data saving** -- Conditional saving of timestep data (per-agent risk components), interface PNG snapshots, and county-level evacuation statistics, depending on user-selected output options.

12. **Stop check** -- If the hurricane has passed through the model domain (all best-track coordinates have been consumed), the model saves final output files (global evacuation statistics, individual agent records) and stops.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:231-309, Go procedure] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### Scheduling Notes

- All processes within a tick are **synchronous**. The order listed above is strictly sequential; no process begins until the previous one completes.
- Citizen-agents execute in NetLogo's default random order within the `ask` primitive. {INFERRED -- standard NetLogo behavior, no explicit ordering override observed in code}
- Each citizen-agent has a staggered decision schedule: `decision-module-turn` is initialized to `random 10`, increments by 1 each tick, and resets to 0 when it reaches `decision-module-interval`. This means agents naturally desynchronize their decision-making over time.
- The `decision-module-interval` is **adaptive**: it halves (more frequent checks) when risk exceeds the `info-up` threshold, and doubles (less frequent checks) when risk falls below the `info-down` threshold. The interval is clamped to the range [1, 32] hours.
- Aggregators update probabilistically (1/3 chance per tick), introducing temporal heterogeneity in the information environment.
- New forecast advisories from the forecaster are published every advisory period (matched to historical NHC advisory timing, typically every 6 hours). Between advisories, the previous forecast remains current.

## 4. Design Concepts

### 4.1 Basic Principles

The model is grounded in the **Protective Action Decision Model (PADM)** framework (Lindell & Perry, 2012). The PADM describes how individuals process threat information through a series of stages: receiving information from environmental and social cues, forming a threat perception, searching for protective actions, and making a decision. The CHIME ABM operationalizes this framework by having citizen-agents collect information from multiple sources (forecasters, broadcasters, aggregators, social networks, environmental cues), form a mental model of the threat (`interpreted-forecast`), calculate a risk assessment (`final-risk-assesment`), and compare that assessment against personal thresholds to decide on protective actions.

The model embodies the central hypothesis that the modern information environment -- characterized by multiple competing information channels with varying accuracy, timeliness, and trustworthiness -- significantly affects evacuation behavior at the population level. An agent-based modeling approach was chosen because evacuation decisions are inherently individual, heterogeneous, and influenced by social networks and information cascading. Aggregate patterns (e.g., evacuation rates, timing distributions) emerge from these individual-level processes in ways that cannot be captured by equation-based models.

The information flow architecture follows a chain: the forecaster produces an authoritative forecast; broadcasters and aggregators translate this for public consumption (with aggregators updating less reliably); citizens receive information from these intermediaries and from their social network peers, blending all sources into a personal interpretation weighted by trust.

[source: README.md:3; CHIME.nlogo:22-31] {DOC_STATED} + {INFERRED from code structure}

### 4.2 Emergence

Aggregate evacuation patterns -- including total evacuation rates, evacuation timing distributions, and the spatial distribution of evacuations relative to storm exposure zones -- emerge from individual-level decision-making. No target evacuation rate is imposed top-down; the aggregate outcome results from thousands of heterogeneous agents independently assessing risk based on their unique thresholds, unique information source portfolios, unique social network positions, and unique decision timing schedules.

Key emergent phenomena include:

- The overall percentage of the population that evacuates
- The temporal profile of evacuations (early vs. late relative to landfall)
- Differential evacuation rates across exposure zones (coastal A-zone vs. inland, within vs. outside wind radii)
- The amplification or dampening of evacuation signals through social networks
- The effect of aggregator unreliability (1/3 update probability) on population-level information quality

These emergent patterns are the primary outputs of interest and are recorded via the Save-Global-Evac-Statistics procedure.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:2373-2516, Save-Global-Evac-Statistics] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 4.3 Adaptation

Citizen-agents adapt their behavior in two principal ways during the simulation:

**1. Adaptive information collection frequency.** The `decision-module-interval` variable controls how often a citizen-agent runs its Decision-Module. This interval adapts based on the agent's current risk assessment:

- When `final-risk-assesment` exceeds the `info-up` threshold, the agent halves its `decision-module-interval` (minimum of 1 hour), meaning it checks for new information and reassesses risk more frequently.
- When `final-risk-assesment` falls below the `info-down` threshold, the agent doubles its `decision-module-interval` (maximum of 32 hours), meaning it checks less frequently.
- This creates a positive feedback loop: as risk perception increases, agents attend more closely to the threat, which can further increase risk perception if new information confirms the threat.

**2. Protective action decisions.** When a citizen-agent's `final-risk-assesment` exceeds its `risk-life-threshold`, the agent evacuates. This is an irreversible binary transition -- once evacuated, agents do not return. When risk is between `risk-property-threshold` and `risk-life-threshold`, agents take "other protective action" (recorded but not spatially modeled) and increase their information collection frequency.

The information that citizen-agents use for these adaptive decisions includes: their `interpreted-forecast` (trust-weighted blend of all forecast sources), official evacuation `orders`, `environmental-cues` (direct sensing of tropical storm winds), their `evac-zone`, and (when enabled) census-derived demographic factors.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:2011-2028] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 4.4 Objectives

Citizen-agents do not explicitly optimize a utility function. Their decision-making follows a **satisficing** paradigm rather than an optimizing one: they evacuate when their perceived risk exceeds a personal threshold, without weighing the costs and benefits of evacuation against alternative actions.

Risk thresholds (`risk-life-threshold`, `risk-property-threshold`, `info-up`, `info-down`) are heterogeneous across agents (drawn from normal distributions during initialization) but remain fixed for each individual agent throughout the simulation. Agents do not adapt their thresholds based on experience or outcomes. There is no explicit objective function being maximized or minimized.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:1962-1999] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 4.5 Learning

Citizen-agents possess a `memory` variable that stores their previous `interpreted-forecast` along with their `self-trust` value. During the next round of forecast processing (Process-Forecasts), this memory is included as one of the information sources that contribute to the new `interpreted-forecast`, weighted by `self-trust`.

However, agents do not engage in learning in the sense of updating their decision rules, thresholds, or trust values over time. The `memory` mechanism serves as **inertia in forecast interpretation** -- an agent's previous belief about the hurricane contributes to its current belief, preventing abrupt changes in interpretation from tick to tick. This is a form of cognitive anchoring rather than adaptive learning.

Specifically, no agent in the model changes its `risk-life-threshold`, `risk-property-threshold`, `self-trust`, `trust-authority`, or trust factors for specific information sources during the simulation. All of these are fixed at initialization.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:1139, 1924 (memory assignment); CHIME.nlogo:2048 (memory used in Process-Forecasts)] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 4.6 Prediction

Citizen-agents form a prediction of the hurricane's future path and intensity through their `interpreted-forecast`, which is a trust-weighted blend of forecasts received from all of their information sources (see Section 7.6, Process-Forecasts). This interpreted forecast contains projected hurricane position (x, y), intensity, cone of uncertainty, and wind radii at hourly intervals extending from the current time to the forecast horizon.

The citizen uses this prediction to estimate two critical quantities:

1. **Time-to-arrival**: The number of hours until the hurricane is forecast to reach its closest approach to the citizen's location.
2. **Spatial proximity**: The distance between the citizen's location and the closest point on the forecast track.

These two quantities -- temporal proximity and spatial proximity -- are the primary inputs to the Gaussian risk function in the Decision-Module (see Section 7.7). The citizen's prediction is imperfect because it is filtered through multiple information intermediaries, each with their own trust weighting, and because the forecast itself contains uncertainty (represented by the cone of uncertainty).

[source: CHIME.nlogo:2038-2122, Process-Forecasts; CHIME.nlogo:1913-1930] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 4.7 Sensing

Citizen-agents sense their environment through both direct and indirect channels:

**Direct sensing:**

- **Environmental cues**: Citizens detect whether they are within the 34-kt wind radius of the actual hurricane, based on their quadrant position relative to the storm center. The model calculates which quadrant (NE, SE, SW, NW) the citizen occupies relative to the hurricane and checks whether the citizen's distance to the storm is less than the 34-kt wind radius for that quadrant. This is a binary variable: `environmental-cues` = 1 if within the wind radius, 0 otherwise. [source: CHIME.nlogo:1896-1905] {CODE_VERIFIED}

**Indirect sensing (mediated through information channels):**

- **Forecast information**: Citizens do NOT access the forecaster's advisory directly. Instead, they receive forecast information through their broadcaster connections, aggregator connections, and social network connections. Each intermediary may introduce delay (aggregators update only 1/3 of the time), noise (through trust-weighted blending), and filtering (random dropping of some forecasts). [source: CHIME.nlogo:2044-2053] {CODE_VERIFIED}

- **Evacuation orders**: Citizens check the official assigned to their county for evacuation orders. This is direct (not mediated by other agents) but is still indirect sensing of the threat, since orders are issued by officials based on their own assessment of coastal patch alerts. [source: CHIME.nlogo:1840-1868] {CODE_VERIFIED}

**Imperfect sensing:**

- **Evacuation zone knowledge**: Citizens know their evacuation zone with approximately 80% accuracy. In the Check-Zone procedure, 80% of agents are assigned a zone based on their actual distance from the coast, while 20% are randomly assigned a zone, modeling the well-documented phenomenon that many residents do not accurately know their evacuation zone. [source: CHIME.nlogo:1170-1187] {CODE_VERIFIED}

**Official sensing:**

- Officials check whether their county's coastal patches have triggered alerts (based on forecast proximity, cone of uncertainty, and wind intensity thresholds). [source: CHIME.nlogo:1840-1868] {CODE_VERIFIED}

There is no explicit sensing cost, delay, or resource constraint for any agent type.

### 4.8 Interaction

Agents interact through four primary mechanisms:

**1. Social network interactions.** Citizen-agents are connected in a scale-free network constructed via modified preferential attachment during initialization. Each citizen maintains a `my-network-list` containing [agent, trust-factor] pairs. During the Process-Forecasts procedure, each citizen queries its network connections for their current `interpreted-forecast` and incorporates these into its own forecast interpretation, weighted by the connection's trust factor. Network connections are bidirectional, fixed after initialization (no dynamic formation or dissolution), and spatially constrained by `network-distance` (default 50 patches). Triadic closure during network formation adds density to the social graph. [source: CHIME.nlogo:1452-1518] {CODE_VERIFIED}

**2. Media interactions.** Citizens subscribe to a random subset of broadcasters via `broadcaster-list` and aggregators via `aggregator-list`, each with randomly assigned trust factors drawn from Uniform[0, 1]. These connections are global -- broadcasters and aggregators serve all subscribing citizens regardless of spatial distance. Media connections are fixed after initialization. [source: CHIME.nlogo:1082-1167] {CODE_VERIFIED}

**3. Information flow chain.** The primary information flow follows a hierarchical chain: Forecaster publishes advisory -> Broadcasters and Aggregators receive and store the interpolated forecast -> Citizens query their broadcaster, aggregator, and social network connections -> Citizens blend all received forecasts using trust-weighted averaging -> Citizens share their interpreted-forecast with network connections who query them in subsequent ticks. This creates a cascading information propagation pattern where information filters through multiple layers before reaching some citizens. [source: CHIME.nlogo:2044-2053] {CODE_VERIFIED}

**4. Official-citizen interaction.** Officials issue county-wide evacuation orders. Each citizen checks the official in its assigned county for the `orders` variable. This interaction is one-directional (official to citizen) and spatially structured by county boundaries. [source: CHIME.nlogo:1840-1868] {CODE_VERIFIED}

Social network interactions are **local** (bounded by `network-distance` during network creation); media and official interactions are **global** (broadcasters and aggregators serve all subscribing citizens regardless of distance; officials serve all citizens in their county).

### 4.10 Collectives

Citizens form social networks through the scale-free network construction algorithm, but these networks do not have collective-level state variables or emergent collective behaviors. There is no group identity, no collective decision-making, and no network-level properties tracked during the simulation. Networks are fixed after initialization -- there is no dynamic formation, dissolution, or rewiring of connections during the simulation.

Counties function as implicit collectives for the purpose of evacuation orders: when an official issues an order, it applies to all citizen-agents within that county. However, counties are not modeled as agents with their own state or behavior; they are spatial groupings defined by GIS data.

## 5. Initialization

The model initialization follows a specific sequence of procedures: `Setup` -> `Load-GIS` -> `Load-Hurricane` -> `Load-Forecasts` -> `Generate-Storm` -> Create agents -> `Social-Network`.

### 5.1 Geographic Initialization

Geographic initialization is handled by the Load-GIS procedure (with a variant Load-GIS-HPC for high-performance computing environments):

1. GIS raster data is loaded for the selected region using the NetLogo GIS extension. Four raster datasets are loaded: elevation (from SRTM .asc files), population density (.asc raster), county boundaries (.asc raster), and county seat locations (.shp vector points).
2. The GIS data envelope is used to compute the coordinate transformation parameters: `grid-cell-size` (degrees per patch), `scale` (nautical miles per patch), and `re0-0` (the geographic coordinate of the grid origin).
3. Patches are assigned state variables from the GIS data: `elev` (elevation), `density` (population density), `county` (county identifier), and derived variables `land?` (true where elevation data exists) and `ocean-patches` / `land-patches` / `coastal-patches` agentsets.
4. Ocean patches are identified as patches where the elevation raster contains no-data values.
5. Coastal patches are identified as land patches that are adjacent (within a neighborhood of 3 patches) to ocean patches.
6. The elevation raster is optionally displayed as a background image when `display-dem?` is enabled.

### 5.2 Agent Initialization

**Citizen-agents (non-census mode):** Citizens are placed on land patches using a population-weighted lottery mechanism. The probability of an agent being placed on a given patch is proportional to that patch's `density` value. For the Hurricane Michael scenario specifically, citizens are constrained to be within 40 grid points of the landfall location and within 6 grid points of the coast, focusing the agent population on the most affected area. Each citizen-agent's state variables are then initialized from their respective distributions (see Section 2.2).

After all agents are created, the Social-Network procedure constructs the citizen-agent social network:

## 7. Submodels

### 7.1 Generate-Storm

**Purpose:** Translates best-track data from geographic coordinates to model grid coordinates and interpolates from 6-hourly to 1-hourly temporal resolution.

**Algorithm:**

1. Read the 6-hourly best-track records from the loaded `best-track-data` list. Each record contains: latitude, longitude, maximum wind intensity (kt), date, hour, 34-kt wind radii (NE, SE, SW, NW quadrants), and 64-kt wind radii (NE, SE, SW, NW quadrants).

2. Convert each record's latitude and longitude to NetLogo grid coordinates using the GIS projection parameters:
   - $x_{\text{grid}} = (lon - lon_{\text{origin}}) / \text{grid-cell-size}$
   - $y_{\text{grid}} = (lat - lat_{\text{origin}}) / \text{grid-cell-size}$

3. For each pair of consecutive 6-hourly records $R_t$ and $R_{t+6}$, linearly interpolate all variables to produce 6 intermediate hourly records. For each variable $V$ and intermediate hour $h \in \{0, 1, 2, 3, 4, 5\}$:
   $$V(t+h) = V(t) + \frac{h}{6} \cdot (V(t+6) - V(t))$$

4. Store the complete interpolated track as the `hurricane-coords-best-track` list, where each element is:
   [x, y, intensity_kt, day, hour, 34kt_NE, 34kt_SE, 34kt_SW, 34kt_NW, 64kt_NE, 64kt_SE, 64kt_SW, 64kt_NW]

**Parameters:** None user-configurable. The procedure is driven entirely by the input best-track data file selected via `which-storm?`.

**Notes:** The interpolation is purely linear, which may produce artifacts for rapidly curving or intensifying storms. Wind radii are also linearly interpolated, which may not accurately represent asymmetric wind field evolution between 6-hourly observations.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:942-1063] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 7.2 Publish-Forecasts

**Purpose:** The forecaster agent publishes an NHC-style advisory matched to the current simulation time.

**Algorithm:**

1. Match the current simulation time (from `clock`) to the appropriate forecast advisory in `forecast-matrix`. Advisories are indexed by issuance time; the most recent advisory that has been "issued" (issuance time <= current time) is selected.

2. For each forecast time point in the advisory, extract: forecast intensity (kt), forecast position (converted to grid x, y coordinates), 34-kt wind radii (4 quadrants), and 64-kt wind radii (4 quadrants).

3. Calculate the cone of uncertainty radius for each forecast lead time by interpolating from the NHC historical error table. Two error tables are used depending on the storm scenario:

**For Irma/Michael storms (newer, smaller cone):**

| Lead time (hours) | 0 | 12 | 24 | 36 | 48 | 72 | 96 | 120 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cone radius (nm) | 0 | 26 | 43 | 56 | 74 | 103 | 151 | 198 |

**For other storms (older advisories, larger cone):**

| Lead time (hours) | 12 | 24 | 36 | 48 | 72 | 96 | 120 |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Cone radius (nm) | 44 | 77 | 111 | 143 | 208 | 266 | 357 |

These cone values are converted from nautical miles to grid cells using the `scale` variable.

4. Output per advisory: Each forecast time point is packaged as [intensity, [x, y], cone_size, [day, hour], [34kt_NE, 34kt_SE, 34kt_SW, 34kt_NW], [64kt_NE, 64kt_SE, 64kt_SW, 64kt_NW]].

[source: CHIME.nlogo:1569-1677] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 7.3 Publish-New-Mental-Model

**Purpose:** Interpolates the discrete forecast advisory (which has data points at 12h, 24h, 36h, etc.) to hourly resolution, creating a continuous hourly forecast product.

**Algorithm:**

1. Take the current forecast from Publish-Forecasts, which contains data at discrete forecast lead times (typically 12, 24, 36, 48, 72, 96, 120 hours).

2. Linearly interpolate between adjacent forecast time points for each variable: wind speed (intensity), position x, position y, and cone error radius. The interpolation follows the same linear scheme as Generate-Storm:
   $$V(t+h) = V(t_1) + \frac{h - t_1}{t_2 - t_1} \cdot (V(t_2) - V(t_1))$$
   where $t_1$ and $t_2$ are the bounding forecast time points and $h$ is the target hour.

3. Combine known current position (from the actual hurricane position at the current tick) with the interpolated forecast future positions to produce a complete hourly forecast track.

4. The resulting interpolated forecast is what broadcasters receive directly and what aggregators receive probabilistically. It is not directly accessible to citizen-agents.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:1680-1804] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 7.4 Coastal-Patches-Alerts

**Purpose:** Coastal patches evaluate whether forecasted storm conditions warrant triggering an evacuation alert.

**Algorithm:**

1. For each patch in `coastal-patches`, the procedure checks three conditions:
   - **Temporal proximity**: The forecasted storm will arrive within `earliest` hours (i.e., the forecast track passes near this patch within the next `earliest` hours).
   - **Spatial proximity**: The patch falls within the forecast cone of uncertainty at the time of closest approach.
   - **Intensity threshold**: The forecast intensity at the time of closest approach exceeds `wind-threshold` kt.

2. If ALL three conditions are simultaneously met, the patch sets `alerts = 1`.

3. Once triggered, alerts persist for the remainder of the simulation (there is no mechanism to cancel alerts).

**Parameters:**

| Parameter | Default | Range | Units | Description |
|-----------|---------|-------|-------|-------------|
| `earliest` | 60 | 12--200 | hours | Maximum forecast lead time for triggering alerts |
| `wind-threshold` | 72 | 70--130 | kt | Minimum forecast intensity required to trigger alerts |

**Notes:** The `earliest` parameter effectively controls how far in advance of storm arrival officials can begin issuing evacuation orders. A value of 60 hours means that alerts can be triggered up to 60 hours before the storm is forecast to reach the coastal patch. The `wind-threshold` of 72 kt corresponds approximately to the boundary between Category 1 and Category 2 hurricanes on the Saffir-Simpson scale.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:1807-1838] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 7.5 Issue-Alerts

**Purpose:** Officials in counties with alerted coastal patches issue evacuation orders to their county's residents.

**Algorithm:**

1. Each official agent checks whether any patch in its county has `alerts = 1`.
2. If alerted patches exist and the official has not yet issued orders (`orders = 0`):
   - Set `orders = 1`
   - Record `when-issued` as the current tick (hours before storm arrival at the official's location)
3. Once issued, orders persist for the remainder of the simulation. There is no mechanism for officials to rescind or modify orders.

**Notes:** The timing of order issuance depends entirely on the Coastal-Patches-Alerts procedure. Officials do not exercise independent judgment; they issue orders whenever their county's coastal patches trigger alerts. This is a simplification of the actual emergency management decision process, where officials consider many factors beyond forecast proximity and intensity.

[source: CHIME.nlogo:1840-1868] {CODE_VERIFIED}

### 7.6 Process-Forecasts

**Purpose:** Citizen-agents collect forecast information from all of their information sources and blend them into a single `interpreted-forecast` using trust-weighted averaging.

**Algorithm:**

1. **Collect forecasts from all sources.** The agent assembles a list of [trust-weight, forecast] pairs from:
   - **Memory**: [self-trust, previous interpreted-forecast] -- the agent's own prior belief
   - **Broadcasters**: For each broadcaster in `broadcaster-list`: [trust-factor, broadcaster's `broadcast`]
   - **Aggregators**: For each aggregator in `aggregator-list`: [trust-factor, aggregator's `info`]
   - **Network connections**: For each connection in `my-network-list`: [trust-factor, connection's `interpreted-forecast`]

2. **Random filtering (bounded rationality).** The collected forecasts are randomly shuffled, and a random number of them are dropped (truncated). This represents the cognitive limitation that agents cannot perfectly process all available information. The number of forecasts retained is random, bounded by the total number collected. {INFERRED -- code comments note "We may want to rethink which forecasts are kept"}

3. **Trust-weighted averaging.** For each forecast variable $V$ at each forecast time $t$, the interpreted value is computed as a trust-weighted average:

$$V_{\text{interpreted}}(t) = \frac{\sum_{i=1}^{n} \text{trust}_i \cdot V_i(t)}{\sum_{j=1}^{n} \text{trust}_j}$$

This weighted averaging is applied independently to: intensity (wind speed), position x, position y, and cone error radius at each hourly time point in the forecast.

4. **Store result.** The blended forecast is stored as the agent's `interpreted-forecast`. The agent's `memory` is also updated to [self-trust, interpreted-forecast] for use in the next decision cycle.

**Notes:** The Process-Forecasts procedure is called both by agents who have not yet evacuated (as part of the Decision-Module) and by agents who have already evacuated (so their network connections can access their updated interpreted-forecast). This means that evacuated agents continue to serve as information conduits within the social network.

### 7.7 Decision-Module

The Decision-Module is the most critical submodel in the CHIME ABM. It is the procedure through which citizen-agents assess their risk and decide whether to take protective action.

**Step 1 -- Calculate time and distance to closest approach.**

Using the citizen-agent's `interpreted-forecast`, the procedure scans all hourly forecast positions to find the forecast hour at which the storm passes closest to the citizen's current location. Let:

- $x$ = hours until the storm reaches the closest approach point (temporal proximity)
- $\text{distance}$ = distance in grid cells between the citizen and the closest approach point (spatial proximity)
- $\text{error\_bars}$ = cone of uncertainty radius at the time of closest approach
- $\text{intensity}$ = forecast wind intensity at the time of closest approach

**Step 2 -- Calculate Gaussian risk.**

The core risk function is a Gaussian (bell curve) centered on a point in time before the storm's arrival, representing the empirical observation that risk perception peaks at a specific lead time (approximately 36 hours before arrival) and diminishes both before and after that peak:

$$\text{risk} = \frac{1}{\text{height} \cdot \sqrt{2\pi}} \cdot \exp\left(-\frac{(x - \text{center})^2}{2 \cdot \text{sd}^2}\right)$$

Where the parameters are:

- $x$ = hours until storm reaches closest point to the citizen-agent
- $\text{center} \sim \mathcal{N}(36, 3)$ -- the peak risk perception occurs approximately 36 hours before the storm's closest approach. Each agent draws its own center value during the risk calculation, introducing per-agent and per-calculation variability.
- $\text{sd} \sim \mathcal{N}(24, 12)$ -- the temporal spread of the risk function. Larger values produce a flatter, broader risk curve; smaller values produce a sharper peak.
- $\text{height}$ -- a scaling factor computed from spatial proximity, evacuation zone, and storm intensity (see below).

**Height calculation:**

The height parameter modulates the amplitude of the Gaussian. Critically, smaller height values produce LARGER risk values because height appears in the denominator:

$$\text{dist\_trk} = \left(\text{scale} \cdot 0.5 \cdot \text{distance} \cdot \frac{0.0011}{\text{error\_bars}}\right) + 0.0011$$

$$\text{height} = \sqrt{\text{dist\_trk} + 0.003 \cdot \text{zone} + 0.000525 \cdot \text{intensity}}$$

Where:

- $\text{distance}$ = distance from the citizen-agent to the closest point on the forecast track (in grid cells)
- $\text{error\_bars}$ = cone of uncertainty radius at the time of closest approach (in grid cells)
- $\text{scale}$ = nautical miles per grid cell (global variable)
- $\text{zone}$ = 0 if the citizen's `evac-zone` is "A" (coastal/most vulnerable), 1 otherwise
- $\text{intensity}$ = 0 if the forecast intensity at closest approach is >= 95 kt (Category 3 or higher on the Saffir-Simpson scale), 1 otherwise

**Interpretation of height components:**

- **Distance/cone ratio ($\text{dist\_trk}$)**: Citizens located within the cone of uncertainty (where distance/error_bars is small) have a small dist_trk, leading to small height, leading to high risk. Citizens far outside the cone have large dist_trk, large height, and low risk.
- **Zone factor (0.003 * zone)**: Coastal citizens (zone "A", zone=0) contribute nothing to height, keeping it smaller and risk higher. Non-coastal citizens (zone=1) add 0.003 to height, slightly reducing risk.
- **Intensity factor (0.000525 * intensity)**: Major hurricanes (>= 95 kt, intensity=0) contribute nothing to height. Weaker storms (intensity=1) add 0.000525, slightly reducing risk. This means citizen-agents perceive higher risk from major hurricanes.

**Step 3 -- Assemble final risk assessment.**

The final risk assessment combines three weighted components:

$$\text{final\_risk} = w_f \cdot \left(1.1 \cdot \mathcal{N}(\text{risk}, 0.5)\right) + w_e \cdot \left(\text{trust\_authority} \cdot 6 \cdot \text{orders} \cdot \text{zone\_factor}\right) + w_c \cdot \left(3 \cdot \text{environmental\_cues}\right)$$

Where:

- $w_f$ = `forc-weight` (user-set, default 1.0, range [0, 2]) -- weight of forecast information
- $w_e$ = `evac-weight` (user-set, default 1.0, range [0, 4]) -- weight of evacuation orders
- $w_c$ = `envc-weight` (user-set, default 1.0, range [0, 6]) -- weight of environmental cues
- $\text{risk}$ = Gaussian risk value from Step 2
- $\mathcal{N}(\text{risk}, 0.5)$ = the risk value with added Gaussian noise (mean = risk, standard deviation = 0.5), representing perceptual uncertainty
- $1.1$ = a 10% upward bias multiplier on the forecast risk component {INFERRED -- appears to be a calibration adjustment}
- $\text{trust\_authority}$ = the citizen's trust in official authority, from Uniform[0, 1]
- $6$ = scaling constant for evacuation orders, ensuring that official orders can produce risk values comparable to forecast risk {INFERRED -- calibration constant}
- $\text{orders}$ = 0 or 1 (whether the official in the citizen's county has issued an evacuation order)
- $\text{zone\_factor}$ = 1 if the citizen's `evac-zone` is "A" (coastal), 0.4 otherwise. This means evacuation orders have 2.5x more impact on coastal residents.
- $3$ = scaling constant for environmental cues {INFERRED -- calibration constant}
- $\text{environmental\_cues}$ = 0 or 1 (whether the citizen is within the 34-kt wind radius)

The three weight parameters ($w_f$, $w_e$, $w_c$) allow users to explore how the relative importance of different information channels affects evacuation outcomes. Equal weights (all 1.0) represent a balanced information environment; higher `forc-weight` represents a forecast-dominated environment; higher `evac-weight` represents an authority-dominated environment; higher `envc-weight` represents a situation where direct environmental experience dominates decision-making.

**Step 4 -- Census demographic modifications.**

When `use-census-data` is set to On, the final risk assessment is modified by census-derived demographic factors. Each factor is controlled by a toggle switch and a magnitude slider:

| Demographic Factor | Direction | Slider Parameter | Effect on `final-risk-assesment` |
|---|---|---|---|
| `kids-under-18?` | Increase | `under-18-assessment-increase` | $+ \text{modifier} \times \text{final\_risk}$ |
| `adults-over-65?` | Decrease | `over-65-assessment-decrease` | $- \text{modifier} \times \text{final\_risk}$ |
| `limited-english?` | Decrease | `limited-english-assessment-decrease` | $- \text{modifier} \times \text{final\_risk}$ |
| `food-stamps?` | Decrease | `foodstamps-assessment-decrease` | $- \text{modifier} \times \text{final\_risk}$ |
| `no-vehicle?` | Decrease | `no-vehicle-assessment-modification` | $- \text{modifier} \times \text{final\_risk}$ |
| `no-internet?` | Decrease | `no-internet-assessment-modification` | $- \text{modifier} \times \text{final\_risk}$ |

The rationale for these directions reflects empirical findings:
- Households with children under 18 tend to perceive higher risk and evacuate more readily (risk increases).
- Households with adults over 65, limited English, food stamp dependence, no vehicle, or no internet face barriers to evacuation (risk perception or capacity decreases), not because they are less at risk, but because they are less likely to receive or act on warning information.

**Step 5 -- Decision thresholds.**

The modified `final-risk-assesment` is compared against the agent's personal thresholds:

| Condition | Action | Side Effect |
|-----------|--------|-------------|
| `final-risk-assesment` > `risk-life-threshold` | **Evacuate** | Agent color set to orange; record ["evacuate" clock counter] in `completed` list; set `evacuated? = true`; set `evac-day` to current day |
| `risk-property-threshold` < `final-risk-assesment` <= `risk-life-threshold` | **Other protective action** | Halve `decision-module-interval` (minimum 1 hour); record action in `completed` |
| `info-up` < `final-risk-assesment` <= `risk-property-threshold` | **Increase information collection** | Halve `decision-module-interval` (minimum 1 hour) |
| `info-down` < `final-risk-assesment` <= `info-up` | **No change** | No modification to `decision-module-interval` |
| `final-risk-assesment` <= `info-down` | **Decrease information collection** | Double `decision-module-interval` (maximum 32 hours) |

Evacuation is an irreversible decision. Once an agent sets `evacuated? = true`, it does not re-enter the protective action decision process in subsequent ticks (though it continues processing forecasts for the benefit of its social network connections).

The adaptive `decision-module-interval` creates a positive feedback loop: higher risk -> more frequent information checking -> potentially updated (and possibly higher) risk -> even more frequent checking. This feedback loop can accelerate evacuation decisions as the storm approaches and information becomes more alarming.

### 7.8 Social-Network

**Purpose:** Creates a scale-free social network among citizen-agents using modified preferential attachment during initialization.

**Algorithm:**

1. **Partner search.** Each citizen-agent searches within `network-distance` (default 50 patches) for other citizen-agents as potential network partners.

2. **Preferential attachment.** Partners are selected probabilistically with probability proportional to the potential partner's current network-list length raised to the `network-size` power:
   $$P(\text{select partner } j) \propto (\text{length of } j\text{'s my-network-list})^{\text{network-size}}$$
   This preferential attachment produces a scale-free (power-law) degree distribution, where a few hub agents have many connections and most agents have few connections, consistent with empirical social network structures.

3. **Triadic closure.** After the initial preferential attachment step, each citizen-agent's network member shares one of its own connections with the citizen. If agent A is connected to agent B, and agent B is connected to agent C, then agent A adds agent C to its network. This triadic closure step increases clustering and creates the "small world" property of realistic social networks.

4. **Trust assignment.** Each network connection is assigned a trust factor drawn from Uniform[0, 1]. This trust factor determines the weight given to that connection's interpreted-forecast during the Process-Forecasts procedure.

5. **Sorting.** Network connections are sorted by trust factor, which may affect which connections are retained during the random filtering step in Process-Forecasts.

**Parameters:**

| Parameter | Default | Range | Units | Description |
|-----------|---------|-------|-------|-------------|
| `network-distance` | 50 | 0--50 | patches | Maximum distance for finding network partners |
| `network-size` | 10 | 1--10 | -- | Power law exponent for preferential attachment |

**Notes:** The `network-size` parameter name is somewhat misleading -- it does not directly control the number of connections per agent. Rather, it controls the degree of preferential attachment: higher values produce more extreme hub-and-spoke topologies (a few agents with very many connections), while lower values produce more uniform degree distributions. The actual number of connections per agent emerges from the algorithm and depends on the spatial distribution of agents and the `network-distance` parameter.

Networks are fixed after initialization. There is no dynamic network evolution, rewiring, or dissolution during the simulation. This is a simplification, as real social networks are dynamic, especially during crisis events when people actively seek new information contacts.

### 7.9 Check-Zone

**Purpose:** Determines each citizen-agent's perceived evacuation zone based on distance from the nearest coastal patch.

**Algorithm:**

1. Calculate the distance from the citizen-agent to the nearest patch in the `coastal-patches` agentset.

2. Assign zone based on distance thresholds:
   - **Zone A** (most coastal, highest vulnerability): distance <= 1.5 grid points
   - **Zone B** (intermediate): 1.5 < distance <= 3 grid points
   - **Zone C** (farther inland): 3 < distance <= 5 grid points
   - **No zone** (inland, not in any evacuation zone): distance > 5 grid points

3. **Imperfect zone knowledge.** With 20% probability, the distance-based zone assignment is overridden with a randomly selected zone. This models the well-documented empirical finding that many residents do not accurately know which evacuation zone they reside in. The 80/20 split represents a calibrated estimate of zone knowledge accuracy.

**Impact on risk calculation:** Evacuation zone affects risk through two pathways in the Decision-Module:
- The `zone` variable in the height calculation (zone "A" = 0, others = 1), which affects the Gaussian risk amplitude.
- The `zone_factor` in the evacuation orders component (zone "A" = 1.0, others = 0.4), which moderates the impact of official evacuation orders.
